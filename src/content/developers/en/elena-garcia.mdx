### Simulated Interview with Elena Garcia

**Q1: What kind of games are you most excited to build right now?**  
I am most excited by **simulation** games with transparent systems that still generate surprising outcomes. I also enjoy **strategy** layers where players can recover from mistakes through planning rather than brute-force optimization.

**Q2: How does your role as Simulation Designer guide your process?**  
As a Simulation Designer, I define the rules of the world before content quantity. I map resource flows, friction points, and decision timing so every strategic move has visible trade-offs and players can understand why a system succeeded or failed.

**Q3: How do you use AI tools in your workflow?**  
I use **Gemini** to explore alternate system architectures and stress-test assumptions in early design docs. I use **Cursor** to quickly implement balancing experiments, telemetry hooks, and simulation parameter tools so iteration cycles stay short and measurable.

**Q4: How do simulation and strategy tags affect your design choices?**  
The **simulation** tag keeps me focused on systemic coherence and long-term consequences. The **strategy** tag pushes me to ensure players always have multiple viable paths, with enough information to make deliberate choices instead of guessing hidden formulas.

**Q5: Can you share a challenge you recently solved?**  
We had a late-game economy that became too stable, so player decisions stopped feeling meaningful. I reworked sink-and-source timing, introduced regional constraints, and used AI-assisted scenario generation to test unusual play patterns before final balancing.

**Q6: What are you aiming to build next?**  
I want to build a strategy simulation where civilian behavior, logistics, and weather all interact without overwhelming the player. The key goal is a clean interface that communicates deep systems clearly, so complexity feels empowering instead of opaque.
