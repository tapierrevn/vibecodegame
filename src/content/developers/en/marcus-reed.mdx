### Simulated Interview with Marcus Reed

**Q1: What kind of games are you most excited to build right now?**  
I am focused on skill-heavy **racing** games with the immediacy of classic **arcade** design. I want every lap to teach players something about risk, route selection, and momentum management without burying that learning under unnecessary complexity.

**Q2: How does your role as Gameplay Programmer influence your approach?**  
As Gameplay Programmer, I obsess over feel before feature count. I iterate on handling curves, traction behavior, and boost timing until inputs feel trustworthy, then layer progression systems that reward consistency and experimentation.

**Q3: What does your AI workflow look like day to day?**  
I use **Claude** to think through tuning logic and to evaluate edge-case behavior in vehicle systems. I use **Cursor** for rapid iteration on gameplay scripts, race-state tools, and debug instrumentation so I can test many handling hypotheses quickly.

**Q4: How do racing and arcade tags guide design choices?**  
The **racing** tag demands clean competitive readability and strong track identity. The **arcade** tag pushes me toward low-friction onboarding, quick rematches, and satisfying speed feedback so players can enjoy the loop immediately while still mastering deeper mechanics.

**Q5: What challenge did you recently solve?**  
One track had a shortcut that dominated all leaderboard routes and reduced strategic variety. I rebalanced split times, adjusted collision forgiveness, and used AI-assisted scenario planning to validate more route diversity without removing high-skill expression.

**Q6: What are you aiming to build next?**  
I want to build a racing toolkit that supports dynamic weather and surface conditions while keeping controls predictable. The goal is to create arcade-accessible racing that still supports long-term mastery and competitive scene depth.
